CHC vs CHPC vs CCEP: Which Healthcare Compliance Certification Should You Get?

By CHC QBank • March 2026 • 9 min read

If you're a healthcare compliance professional weighing certification options, you've likely encountered three acronyms: CHC (Certified in Healthcare Compliance), CHPC (Certified in Healthcare Privacy Compliance), and CCEP (Certified Compliance & Ethics Professional). All three are respected credentials, but they serve different purposes and test different bodies of knowledge.

Here's a clear, honest comparison to help you decide which certification aligns with your career — and how to prepare.

Exam Content: Where They Diverge

This is where your study strategy — and career direction — diverges significantly.

CHC: Healthcare Compliance Broadly

The CHC exam covers the full scope of healthcare compliance. Its seven domains span compliance program administration, monitoring and auditing, investigations, education and training, enforcement and discipline, standards and policies, and screening. You need deep knowledge of the Anti-Kickback Statute, Stark Law, False Claims Act, the OIG's Seven Elements, and how all of these work together in a healthcare organization's compliance program.

The CHC is the most popular compliance certification in healthcare and the one most frequently listed in compliance officer and compliance director job postings.

CHPC: Healthcare Privacy Specifically

The CHPC is a specialist credential focused exclusively on healthcare privacy. The exam tests in-depth knowledge of the HIPAA Privacy Rule, the HIPAA Security Rule, breach notification requirements, state privacy laws, patient rights under HIPAA, and the intersection of privacy with research, marketing, and fundraising in healthcare settings.

If your role is primarily focused on HIPAA compliance, privacy impact assessments, breach response, or if you're a Health Information Management (HIM) professional, the CHPC may be more directly relevant to your daily work than the CHC.

CCEP: Cross-Industry Compliance

The CCEP is administered by the Society of Corporate Compliance and Ethics (SCCE) through the same Compliance Certification Board. Unlike the CHC and CHPC, it is not healthcare-specific. The CCEP tests general compliance and ethics principles applicable across industries — the Federal Sentencing Guidelines, risk assessment methodology, compliance program design, investigations, and codes of conduct.

If you work in healthcare but want a credential that's portable to other industries (financial services, manufacturing, technology), or if your organization operates across multiple regulated sectors, the CCEP provides that flexibility.

Which Should You Choose?

Choose CHC if: you work in healthcare compliance in any capacity — as a compliance officer, analyst, manager, or director — and your role involves managing or supporting a compliance program that addresses fraud and abuse, billing compliance, physician arrangements, and regulatory adherence. This is the default choice for most healthcare compliance professionals.

Choose CHPC if: your primary responsibility is healthcare privacy — HIPAA compliance, breach management, privacy impact assessments, or health information management. Many privacy officers hold both the CHC and CHPC.

Choose CCEP if: you want an industry-agnostic compliance credential, you work in a multi-industry organization, or you're early in your compliance career and want maximum portability across sectors.

The good news: there is significant overlap between all three exams. The Federal Sentencing Guidelines, compliance program fundamentals, investigation procedures, and risk assessment methodologies are tested across all of them. A strong foundation in compliance program administration prepares you for any of these certifications.

Can You Hold Multiple Certifications?

Yes, and many senior compliance professionals do. The most common combination is CHC + CHPC for those who manage both general compliance and privacy programs in a healthcare setting. Adding the CCEP on top signals broad expertise across compliance disciplines.

The CCB allows you to use continuing education credits toward multiple certifications simultaneously, which makes maintaining more than one credential manageable.

Difficulty: What Candidates Report

All three exams are considered challenging, with similar formats and scoring methodologies. The CCB doesn't publish official pass rates for any of them. Anecdotally, the CHC pass rate is estimated around 65%, and candidates report that all three exams are heavily scenario-based — testing your ability to apply regulations to realistic situations, not just recall facts.

Most successful candidates study for 4–8 weeks, focusing on practice questions that mirror the exam format. Passive reading of textbooks is consistently reported as insufficient preparation.

How to Prepare for Any of These Exams

Regardless of which certification you choose, the most effective preparation follows the same formula: master the foundational regulations and guidelines, then test yourself relentlessly with scenario-based practice questions. Reading alone isn't enough — you need to practice applying knowledge under timed conditions.

For the CHC specifically, your preparation should center on the OIG's Seven Elements, the major fraud and abuse statutes (AKS, Stark, FCA), the 60-Day Overpayment Rule, HIPAA fundamentals, and investigation/remediation procedures.

Final Thought

All three certifications are respected in the compliance community. Don't overthink the choice — pick the one that most closely aligns with your current role and career goals, then invest your energy in actually preparing well. The credential on your resume matters far less than the knowledge you bring to your organization every day.

If you're going for the CHC, you're choosing the most widely recognized healthcare compliance credential in the industry. And with the right preparation, it's very achievable.
