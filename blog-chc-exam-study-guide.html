How to Pass the CHC Exam in 2026: A Complete Study Guide

By CHC QBank • March 2026 • 10 min read

If you're a healthcare compliance professional preparing for the Certified in Healthcare Compliance (CHC) exam, you know the stakes. Certification opens doors to higher salaries, stronger credibility, and leadership roles at health systems, hospitals, and payer organizations. But the exam itself can feel daunting — especially when you're studying on top of a demanding full-time compliance workload.

With a pass rate hovering around 65%, the CHC exam is not a formality. It requires real preparation. Here's a proven approach to pass it on your first attempt.

What the CHC Exam Actually Tests

The CHC exam is administered by the Compliance Certification Board (CCB), an affiliate of the Health Care Compliance Association (HCCA). It consists of 120 multiple-choice questions — 100 scored and 20 unscored pilot questions — delivered over 2 hours.

The exam is built around the HCCA Detailed Content Outline (DCO), which defines seven domains of healthcare compliance. This isn't a test of pure memorization — it's heavily scenario-based. You'll be given real-world compliance situations and asked to identify the correct course of action based on federal regulations, OIG guidance, and industry best practices.

Understanding the relative weight of each domain is critical for allocating your study time effectively. The two largest domains — Compliance Program Administration (19%) and Monitoring, Auditing & Internal Reporting (18%) — should consume the bulk of your preparation.

The 6-Week Study Plan That Works

Most candidates who pass report studying for 4–8 weeks. Here's how to structure that time effectively:

Weeks 1–2: Build Your Regulatory Foundation

Start with the core legal and regulatory framework. Read and understand the OIG's Seven Elements of an Effective Compliance Program — this is the backbone of the entire exam. Study the Federal Sentencing Guidelines and how they define an "effective compliance and ethics program." Review the major federal healthcare fraud and abuse statutes: the Anti-Kickback Statute, Stark Law, False Claims Act, and the 60-Day Overpayment Rule.

Don't memorize — focus on understanding the purpose behind each law and how it applies to real compliance situations. Know the safe harbors for AKS, the exceptions for Stark, and the qui tam provisions of the FCA.

Weeks 3–4: Deep Dive Into High-Weight Domains

Focus on the areas that carry the most exam weight. Compliance Program Administration covers everything from risk assessments and compliance committee structure to work plan development and board reporting. Monitoring, Auditing & Internal Reporting tests your knowledge of audit methodology, the 60-Day Overpayment Rule in practice, and voluntary self-disclosure protocols.

Also dedicate significant time to Investigations & Remedial Measures (16%) — expect scenario-based questions about discovering potential violations, conducting investigations, implementing corrective action plans, and documentation requirements.

Weeks 5–6: Practice Questions and Simulated Exams

This is where most people's study plans fall short. Reading regulations is not enough — you need to practice applying that knowledge under exam-like conditions. Aim for at least 20–30 scenario-based questions per day. Review every explanation, even for questions you got right. The rationale behind wrong answers is just as valuable as understanding why the right answer is correct.

In your final week, take full-length timed practice exams. The real exam gives you 2 hours for 120 questions — that's exactly one minute per question. Practice under those constraints so test day feels familiar.

7 High-Yield Topics You Must Know

  1. OIG's Seven Elements — Know each element cold. Understand how they map to the Federal Sentencing Guidelines. Expect 15–20 questions that touch on these directly or indirectly.
  2. Anti-Kickback Statute & Safe Harbors — Understand what constitutes illegal remuneration, the intent requirement (compared to Stark's strict liability), and the key safe harbors: personal services, space rental, employee, and fair market value.
  3. Stark Law & Exceptions — Know the designated health services, the prohibition on physician self-referrals, and the critical exceptions (in-office ancillary services, fair market value, bona fide employment).
  4. False Claims Act — Treble damages, qui tam/whistleblower provisions, the knowledge standard (actual knowledge, deliberate ignorance, reckless disregard), and how it intersects with the 60-Day Rule.
  5. The 60-Day Overpayment Rule — This is heavily tested. Know the timeline: once an overpayment is "identified," you have 60 days to report and return it or face FCA liability. Understand what "identified" means and the look-back period.
  6. HIPAA Privacy & Security — Covered entities vs. business associates, the minimum necessary standard, breach notification requirements (including the 60-day notification timeline and HHS reporting thresholds), and enforcement mechanisms.
  7. Investigation Procedures — How to scope and conduct an internal investigation, when to involve legal counsel, attorney-client privilege considerations, corrective action plan development, and documentation requirements.

Common Mistakes That Cost People the Exam

Relying only on textbook reading. The CHC exam tests application, not recall. You can know every statute by heart and still fail if you haven't practiced applying that knowledge to scenarios where multiple answers seem plausible.

Studying everything equally. Not all domains carry equal weight. Compliance Program Administration (19%) and Monitoring & Auditing (18%) together represent over a third of the exam. Screening & Evaluation (6%) is important but much smaller. Allocate your time proportionally.

Ignoring the "wrong" answers. The CHC exam uses well-crafted distractors. Understanding why each incorrect answer is wrong deepens your comprehension and helps you eliminate similar traps on exam day.

Not practicing under timed conditions. One minute per question is faster than it sounds. If you've never practiced answering compliance scenarios under time pressure, test day will feel very different from your study sessions at home.

Why Scenario-Based Practice Is the Key

The single biggest difference between candidates who pass and those who don't is the quality of their practice. Reading a regulation tells you the rule. A scenario-based question teaches you how to apply the rule when you're presented with a realistic compliance situation and four plausible-sounding options.

The CHC exam doesn't ask "What is the Anti-Kickback Statute?" It asks: "A physician group is offered free EHR training by a device manufacturer in exchange for purchasing their equipment. The compliance officer should..." That's a different skill entirely.

This is why passive studying — reading textbooks, highlighting notes, watching lectures — only gets you partway there. Active, question-based practice with immediate feedback is what builds the pattern recognition you need to perform under exam conditions.

Don't Skip the Small Domain: Screening & Evaluation

At only 6% of the exam, Screening & Evaluation is the smallest domain. Some candidates are tempted to skip it entirely. Don't. That 6% represents approximately 6 scored questions — and on an exam where the pass/fail margin can be razor-thin, those questions could make or break your score.

This domain covers OIG exclusion screening (checking the OIG LEIE and SAM databases), background check requirements, vendor and contractor due diligence, and ongoing monitoring of excluded individuals. It's relatively straightforward material — a few hours of focused study can lock in those points.

E6(R3) and the Evolving Compliance Landscape

Healthcare compliance is not static. The regulatory environment continues to evolve with new OIG guidance, enforcement priorities, and technology-driven challenges. While the CHC exam is based on established principles and current regulations, staying aware of trends — like the DOJ's increased focus on data analytics in investigations, the expansion of telehealth compliance requirements, and evolving cybersecurity mandates — makes you a stronger compliance professional beyond the exam.

Your Next Step

The CHC exam is within reach. The candidates who pass aren't necessarily the ones who study the most — they're the ones who study the right way. Build your regulatory foundation, focus your time on high-weight domains, and test yourself relentlessly with scenario-based practice.
